 State Space design


 What to say: "This is the most important design decision in the whole project. 
 We have to convert continuous market state into discrete buckets so we can use a Q-table. 
 Three dimensions:

Time bins — finer near expiry because that's where things change fastest. Notice the spacing: 0–5 days is one bin, but 175–215 is a single bin too. Near expiry the option's delta changes quickly with stock price (gamma is high), so we need more resolution there.
Moneyness S/K — also finer near 1.0 (at-the-money) because that's where the option value curves the most.
Position error — this is the key insight. Instead of tracking 'what's my raw position?', we track 'how far am I from the Black-Scholes target?'. This collapses the hedge decision to one dimension: if the error is small, do nothing; if large, trade to reduce it.

The actions are 7 possible position changes. We have small adjustments (±2.5%, ±8%) for fine corrections, big ones (±20%) for snap-backs after price jumps, and crucially 0.0 — do nothing. Without that, no no-trade band is possible."

This function takes a continuous (price, position, time) and gives back the three integer bucket indices, plus the BS delta for use in the reward later.
 The total state space is 10 × 9 × 9 = 810 states, times 7 actions = 5,670 Q-values to learn. Tiny — a deep network would be massive overkill here

Section 5 Hedge simulator

 There are three simulators (sim_Q, sim_bs, sim_band) — they all follow the same accounting structure.
What to say: "All three follow the same pattern. We're tracking the writer's P&L:

Start with cash = V0 (we collected the option premium).
At each step: choose a new position (the policy differs here), buy/sell stock, pay transaction costs.
At maturity: liquidate the remaining stock, pay the option payoff to the holder.

The only thing that differs between the three is how new_pos is chosen:

sim_bs rebalances to BS delta every single step
sim_band only rebalances if |pos − delta| > half_width
sim_Q looks up the action in the Q-table and applies it"

Key teaching point: "The terminal P&L is the writer's profit. Negative is bad — it means the writer lost money. 
BS gets a mean P&L of −6, meaning they paid $6 in costs that ate their entire premium plus some."

sharpe — risk-adjusted return

 (a) ε-greedy exploration. With probability ε, pick a random action; otherwise, pick the best one we know about. 
 ε starts at 0.4 (lots of exploration) and decays to 0.03 (mostly exploit). This is how we balance trying new things vs using what we've learned.

(b) The reward function — this is where the magic is. Three terms:

The intuition: if you're 5% off the BS hedge, your P&L will fluctuate by an amount proportional to that. 
We punish the expected variance directly.
At the last step: subtract the option payoff max(Sₜ − K, 0). This makes the agent care about the final outcome.

The hyperparameter λ is everything. Low λ → agent ignores risk, just minimizes costs (results in not hedging). 
High λ → agent ignores cost, hedges aggressively (becomes BS). Somewhere in between is optimal — we sweep this.
(c) The Q-update. Standard Bellman equation: Q(s,a) ← Q(s,a) + α[r + γ·max Q(s', ·) − Q(s,a)]. 
The learning rate α shrinks with the visit count, so well-explored cells stop changing — this gives stable convergence

Double Q-learning

What to say: "This is the famous Double Q-learning algorithm by Hasselt (2010). Standard Q-learning has a bias: because we take max over a noisy Q-table, we systematically overestimate values. Double Q fixes this by maintaining two tables. One picks the action, the other evaluates it. The biases cancel.
Plot twist: in our problem, this doesn't help. We tested it and it's actually worse. Why? Three reasons:

Each table only gets half the updates → effectively half the training budget.
Our reward is mostly deterministic (TC and payoff aren't noisy), so there's not much overestimation bias to fix in the first place.
Our warm start already gives sensible Q-values.

This is a useful negative result — 'a standard improvement that doesn't transfer here.' 
Worth showing in the thesis."

Section 11

We train on one set of paths but evaluate on a completely different set (different seed). 
This is OOS testing, the gold standard. If our agent only memorized the training paths, it would fail here.
The λ-sweep is how we find the right risk/cost trade-off. 
We don't pick the highest Sharpe blindly — we pick the lowest TC subject to std ≤ BS std. This makes the comparison fair: 'at the same risk level, who trades less?'
The 'extended training' step is fine-tuning. After the broad search finds a good λ, we polish that agent with a smaller learning rate and less exploration to lock in the policy.
The Double Q step is the ablation. Same λ, same total episodes, just different update rule.
